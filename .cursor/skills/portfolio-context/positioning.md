# Positioning — Marcos Lott

This file defines the strategic narrative of the portfolio. It should guide copywriting, visual hierarchy, case study selection, homepage sections, and how the AI agents decide what to emphasize.

The portfolio should not simply prove that Marcos can code. It should prove that he can own complex product surfaces, reason about architecture, communicate trade-offs, and turn engineering work into business and user outcomes.

---

## Core Differentiator

Marcos operates at the intersection of **frontend architecture**, **fintech product impact**, **real-time systems**, and **AI-assisted engineering craft**.

He is not positioned as a generic React developer. He is a product-minded frontend engineer who has worked on trading and investment experiences where correctness, reliability, state management, performance, and trust matter. His work connects UI decisions to business outcomes: revenue, order volume, user autonomy, platform stability, and engineering velocity.

A concise version:

> Marcos builds high-trust financial interfaces and developer workflows that turn complex systems into clear, reliable user experiences.

A more personal version:

> Marcos is a frontend engineer becoming a systems-minded technical leader — someone who moves fast, thinks in architecture, communicates clearly, and uses AI as leverage without outsourcing judgment.

## Positioning Pillars

### 1. High-trust frontend engineering

Marcos's strongest professional territory is frontend work where reliability matters. Trading screens, investment order flows, market data, and financial UX are not just interface problems; they require correctness, resilience, and careful state modeling.

The portfolio should show:

- Real-time financial interfaces.
- Complex order flows.
- State management decisions.
- Reliability patterns such as circuit-breaker and exponential backoff.
- Strong user trust and business constraints.

Avoid framing frontend as only visual implementation. For Marcos, frontend is the layer where product, systems, and user trust meet.

### 2. Product impact, not just technical output

The portfolio should repeatedly connect engineering work to outcomes. UCITS Web is the clearest proof: revenue, order volume, adoption, and product expansion.

The portfolio should show:

- $161k in UCITS Web commissions within 4 months.
- 5,615 UCITS Web orders in 4 months.
- 33% of total UCITS revenue attributed to web.
- 418% stronger launch performance than Options Web in the comparable window.
- 200k+ monthly orders and $620M+ monthly volume supported by investment flows.

This should be presented with restraint. The numbers are strong enough; the copy does not need to oversell them.

### 3. Architectural clarity

Marcos should be positioned as someone who makes systems easier to understand. His work should highlight clear boundaries, typed state, documented flows, reusable components, and explicit trade-offs.

The portfolio should demonstrate:

- How he thinks before building.
- Why certain architectural decisions were made.
- What constraints existed.
- What trade-offs were accepted.
- How future maintainability improved.

A good portfolio page should make the reader feel: "I would trust this person inside a messy production codebase."

### 4. AI as engineering leverage

AI should be part of Marcos's positioning, but not as hype. The message should be: Marcos uses AI to increase engineering leverage while keeping human judgment, quality gates, and architecture in control.

The portfolio should show:

- Cursor and Claude used in real workflows.
- AI-assisted component migration.
- Custom agents or structured prompts.
- Validation and review as part of the workflow.
- Repository architecture as a prerequisite for good AI output.

Avoid presenting AI as magic. Present it as craft: context design, constraints, review, iteration, and quality control.

### 5. Communication as technical leverage

Marcos's content creation should be treated as a strategic extension of his engineering profile. The point is not to become an influencer; the point is to compound clarity, technical authority, and career surface area.

The portfolio should show:

- LinkedIn content in English for international reach.
- Instagram content in Portuguese through the lott.dev direction.
- Themes around React, frontend architecture, system design, AI-assisted workflows, and career development.
- Ability to explain complex engineering ideas in accessible language.

Content should appear as evidence of thought process and communication, not as a separate marketing gimmick.

### 6. Full-stack and systems awareness

Marcos's core brand should remain frontend/product engineering, but the portfolio should show that he understands the surrounding system: APIs, data flows, backend constraints, telemetry, infrastructure, and business rules.

The portfolio can use:

- Fórmula Tesla full-stack project management platform.
- Real-time telemetry dashboard.
- Dito Flutter SDK.
- Node.js, Express, PostgreSQL experience.
- WebSocket/EventSource experience.
- Financial platform integrations.

The message should be range without dilution: frontend is the center, systems thinking is the multiplier.

The portfolio should feel:

- Clear.
- Direct.
- Mature.
- Calmly confident.
- Technical without being dense.

- Startup buzzword overload.
- Overclaiming seniority without evidence.
- Generic phrases like "passionate about technology".
- Treating AI as a trend instead of a specific workflow advantage.
- Making the portfolio feel like a LinkedIn profile copied into a website.

## Content Creation Positioning

Content should be framed as a continuation of engineering, not a side identity detached from the work.

Recommended framing:

> I use content to clarify what I am learning and practicing: frontend architecture, system design, AI-assisted workflows, and the shift from simply writing code to understanding systems.

Topics that fit the brand:

- React and frontend architecture in the AI era.
- Repository structure as a prerequisite for useful AI assistance.
- System design for frontend engineers.
- Engineering fundamentals that still matter with AI.
- Lessons from fintech and trading interfaces.
- Career growth toward international engineering roles.
- Communication, technical leadership, and product thinking.

Topics to avoid or treat carefully:

- Generic motivational content.
- Tool hype without technical depth.
- Hot takes that do not connect to Marcos's actual experience.
- Content that makes him look like a creator first and engineer second.
